Visual Audit: Kala Ghoda

Digital Heritage Survey 2025

The Kala Ghoda Visual Audit is an interactive digital survey documenting the architectural evolution of Mumbai’s premier art district. Using web-based mapping technology, this project catalogs the diverse stylistic lineage of the precinct—from the demolition of the Fort walls in the 1860s to the Art Deco boom of the 1930s.

This tool serves as an open-access archive for students, urban planners, and historians to analyze the spatial relationship between the city's colonial past and its living heritage.

The Precinct

Named after the black stone statue of King Edward VII (the "Black Horse") that once stood at the node, Kala Ghoda is a unique crescent-shaped precinct. It represents one of the finest concentrations of heritage structures in India, transitioning seamlessly from Victorian Neo-Gothic public buildings to early 20th-century Indo-Saracenic museums and later Edwardian commercial blocks.

Stylistic Layering

Filter the map by architectural style (Gothic, Art Deco, Indo-Saracenic) to see how different eras of construction cluster in specific zones.

Time Travel

Use the temporal slider to strip away modern developments and view the precinct as it existed in 1890, 1920, or 1950.

1883 Historical Map

Overlay a high-fidelity raster scan of the 1883 Survey of India map to compare historical street alignments with the modern urban fabric.

3D Morphology

Explore the built form in 3D to understand the relationship between street widths, building heights, and the famous 'Rampart Row'.

Methodology

This audit utilizes vector mapping based on OpenMapTiles, overlaid with custom GeoJSON datasets derived from historical records. Each data point is categorized by construction year, architect (where known), and architectural classification. The "Invisible Ramparts" layer traces the exact coordinates of the original Bombay Fort walls, demolished in 1862, providing critical context to the precinct's layout.
